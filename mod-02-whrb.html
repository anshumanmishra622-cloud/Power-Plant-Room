MOD-02 / WHRB System

From kiln gas to 490°C steam.

Four sponge iron kilns send their exhaust gas through these WHRB units instead of a stack. No fuel is burned here — every kilojoule in the steam was already on its way out as waste heat.

Natural circulation loop

No circulation pump — density does the work.

Cooler water in the downcomers is denser than the steam-water mixture in the risers. That density difference alone drives the loop:

DRUM

Steam Drum

Saturated water separates from steam. Steam exits to the superheaters; water feeds the downcomers.

Downcomers

Unheated, large-bore pipe carrying cool saturated water down to the bottom headers. SA106GR.B, 88.9–219.1mm.

EW05

Water Wall Panels

Membrane tube panels absorb radiant kiln-gas heat directly. Boiling begins here. 51×4.5mm, SA210GR.A1.

Risers

Carry the lighter steam-water mixture back up to the drum. SA234WPB, 88.9–114.3mm.

Getting to 490°C

Economiser → Superheaters

EW29–31

Economiser

Sits in the cooler tail end of the gas path. Preheats feedwater before it enters the drum — squeezes out heat that would otherwise be lost, and reduces the thermal shock on the drum. 38.1×4.06mm, SA210GR.A1.

EW40

Primary Superheater

First superheat stage, positioned where gas is hotter. Takes saturated steam from the drum and starts raising it past saturation temperature. SA210GR.A1.

EW41

Secondary Superheater

Finishing stage — brings steam up to the full 490°C delivery temperature. Higher-grade material for the higher metal temperature here. SA213T11.

EW07 & EW08 require PWHT (post-weld heat treatment), electrode E7018-1 — full schedule in MOD-05.

The thing that gets people in the exam

Swell & Shrinkage

Swell

Triggered by a sudden drop in drum pressure — typically a sharp rise in steam demand. The saturated water flashes, throwing off a burst of extra bubbles. The water-steam mixture's bulk volume jumps even though total water mass is falling. The level gauge reads a rise — at the exact moment the boiler actually needs more feed water, not less.

Shrinkage

The mirror image: a sudden rise in drum pressure (load drop, firing cut back) collapses bubbles back into solution. Mixture volume contracts. The level gauge reads a fall — even though water mass hasn't actually dropped.

This is exactly why single-element level control (level only) is dangerous on a unit this size: it would respond to swell by cutting feedwater, worsening a real shortage. Three-element control — drum level, steam flow, and feedwater flow, in a cascade PID arrangement — uses the steam/feedwater flow mismatch to anticipate the correct feed response instead of waiting on a level signal that's lying to you for a few seconds.

Quick check

Q&A — WHRB System

What actually drives natural circulation if there's no pump? CONCEPT
Density difference between the unheated, denser water in the downcomers and the heated, lighter steam-water mixture in the risers. That difference creates a continuous driving head — no mechanical pump required.
Why does swell happen on a sudden steam demand increase, specifically? MECHANISM
Higher demand pulls more steam out of the drum, which drops drum pressure. Lower pressure means the saturated water is now superheated relative to the new, lower saturation temperature — so it flashes into extra bubbles instantly, inflating the mixture's bulk volume.
Why is the economiser placed at the cool end of the gas path, not the hot end? DESIGN
It only needs to preheat feedwater, a relatively low-temperature task — putting it where gas has already given up most of its heat to the water walls and superheaters still recovers meaningful energy, while keeping the highest-grade tubes where the highest gas temperatures actually are.
Why does the superheater come in two stages instead of one? DESIGN
Splitting it allows an attemperation (desuperheating spray) stage in between, giving operators a control point to trim final steam temperature precisely to 490°C rather than relying purely on firing rate and gas-side heat transfer.
