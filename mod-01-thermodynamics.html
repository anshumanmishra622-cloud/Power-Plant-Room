MOD-01 / Thermodynamics

Rankine Cycle & the steam loop.

Every WHRB unit on this plant is running the same four-step loop a textbook draws on a T-s diagram. Knowing it cold is what separates reading a gauge from understanding what the gauge is telling you.

The four processes

One loop, four strokes.

1 → 2

Pump

Isentropic compression. Feed water pushed from condenser pressure up to boiler pressure. Tiny work input — liquid is nearly incompressible.

→
2 → 3

Boiler

Constant-pressure heat addition. Economiser preheats, evaporator turns water to saturated steam, superheater pushes it to 490°C.

→
3 → 4

Turbine

Isentropic expansion. Steam gives up enthalpy as shaft work. Pressure and temperature both fall sharply.

→
4 → 1

Condenser

Constant-pressure heat rejection. Exhaust steam gives up latent heat to cooling water, returns to saturated liquid.

↻ state 1 feeds back into the pump — the loop closes here.

Vocabulary that gets mixed up

hf, hfg, hg — read the subscript.

TermMeaningPhysically
hfEnthalpy of saturated liquidSensible heat already in the water at that pressure, before any boiling starts
hfgLatent heat of vaporisationHeat needed to convert that saturated liquid fully to saturated vapour — no temperature rise, only phase change
hgEnthalpy of saturated vapourhg = hf + hfg — total heat content of dry saturated steam

As boiler pressure rises toward the critical point, hfg shrinks — less latent heat is needed because the liquid and vapour phases are converging. At the critical point, hfg = 0.

Two points people confuse

Critical Point vs. Triple Point.

Critical Point

The state where liquid and vapour become indistinguishable — no boiling, no meniscus, just one supercritical fluid.

374.15°CCritical Temperature (water)
~225.6kg/cm²Critical Pressure (water)
Triple Point

The single state where solid, liquid and vapour all coexist in equilibrium at once.

0.01°CTriple Point Temperature
0.00611barTriple Point Pressure

This plant runs at 69 kg/cm² normal working pressure — well below the critical point, which is exactly why there's a distinct boiling process happening inside the drum at all. A supercritical boiler wouldn't have a drum.

Why pressure and temperature matter

The efficiency ceiling.

Thermal efficiency of the cycle is net work out over heat in:

η = (Wturbine − Wpump) / Qboiler

The theoretical ceiling on that number is set by the Carnot limit, ηmax = 1 − Tc/Th, using absolute temperatures of heat rejection and heat addition. Push Th up — raise boiler pressure and superheat temperature — and the ceiling rises with it. That's the entire reason superheating to 490°C instead of feeding the turbine saturated steam matters: it raises Th, raises efficiency, and as a side benefit keeps the turbine's last-stage steam dry enough to avoid blade erosion from moisture.

Quick check

Q&A — Thermodynamics

Why does superheating the steam improve cycle efficiency, not just turbine safety? CONCEPT
It raises the average temperature at which heat is added during the 2→3 process. A higher heat-addition temperature pushes the Carnot ceiling up directly — the dryness-fraction benefit at the turbine exhaust is a separate, additional win, not the main mechanism.
If hfg is the latent heat, why does it fall as pressure increases? CONCEPT
Higher pressure pushes saturation temperature up, which pushes hf and hg closer together as both approach the critical point. At the critical point the two phases merge entirely, so hfg → 0.
Is the pump process in a Rankine cycle isothermal or isentropic? DEFINITION
Isentropic (constant entropy), not isothermal. Liquid water is nearly incompressible, so the temperature rise across the pump is small but real — entropy stays constant in the ideal case, temperature doesn't.
What's the practical difference between the critical point and the triple point on a P-T diagram? DEFINITION
The triple point is the bottom end of the saturation curve, where all three phases meet at one specific low-pressure, low-temperature state. The critical point is the top end, where the liquid-vapour line simply terminates — beyond it, "boiling" stops being a meaningful process.
