What is dryness fraction, and why does it matter at the turbine's last stage? THERMO
Dryness fraction (x) is the mass ratio of vapour to total mass in a wet steam mixture. As steam expands through the turbine its pressure and temperature fall, and if it crosses too far into the wet region, water droplets form and erode the last-stage blades. Superheating keeps x close enough to 1 through the early stages to delay that crossing.
Distinguish sensible heat from latent heat in the context of the evaporator. THERMO
Sensible heat raises water's temperature without changing phase (this is h_f building up). Latent heat is absorbed entirely in changing phase from liquid to vapour at constant temperature (h_fg). The evaporator section is where latent heat addition happens — temperature holds at saturation temperature the whole time.
Why does specific volume of saturated steam fall as pressure rises? THERMO
Higher pressure packs the same mass of steam molecules into less space — density rises, specific volume falls. This is also why higher-pressure steam lines can carry the same mass flow through a smaller pipe diameter.
What's "degree of superheat" and why track it separately from absolute steam temperature? THERMO
It's the difference between actual steam temperature and the saturation temperature at that same pressure. Tracking it separately matters because saturation temperature itself shifts with pressure — 490°C steam at 69 kg/cm² has a very different margin above saturation than the same 490°C would at a different pressure.
What do the steam drum's internal cyclone separators actually do? SYSTEMS
They spin the incoming steam-water mixture from the risers, using centrifugal force to fling the heavier water droplets to the outside wall while drier steam continues to the outlet — reducing moisture carryover into the superheater.
This plant uses natural circulation. When would a boiler need forced circulation instead? SYSTEMS
At very high pressures approaching the critical point, the density difference between water and steam shrinks toward zero — there's not enough driving head left for natural circulation to work, so a circulating pump becomes necessary.
What's the difference between continuous and intermittent blowdown? SYSTEMS
Continuous blowdown runs constantly at a small, controlled rate from the drum's water surface, holding dissolved solids (TDS) at a steady target. Intermittent blowdown is a periodic manual or timed dump from the bottom header, aimed at clearing settled sludge rather than controlling TDS.
Where does a safety valve's set pressure typically sit relative to NWP and MWP? SYSTEMS
Above normal working pressure but at or below maximum working pressure — close enough above NWP to not lift during normal operation, but guaranteed to lift before the drum or any pressure part reaches MWP.
What's the purpose of an economiser bypass damper? SYSTEMS
It routes flue gas around the economiser during low-load or startup conditions, when gas temperatures are low enough that cooling them further risks acid dew-point corrosion on the economiser tubes.
What's the purpose of a drum low-low level trip? SAFETY
It force-trips the firing/heat input the instant drum level falls below a safe threshold — protecting the water wall tubes from running dry and overheating, which can cause tube rupture within seconds once the water-side cooling is lost.
List the primary causes of boiler tube failure. SAFETY
Overheating from low water level or internal scale deposits, corrosion from poor water chemistry or dissolved oxygen, erosion from high-velocity ash or soot particles, and mechanical fatigue from repeated thermal cycling or vibration.
Why is firing with even momentarily low water level treated as a serious trip condition, not just an alarm? SAFETY
Water-wall tubes depend on internal water flow to carry heat away from the metal. The moment a section runs dry, the tube wall temperature can climb past its design limit within seconds, weakening the metal toward rupture — there usually isn't time for a human response, which is why it's hard-wired to trip automatically.
What is PWHT, and why do only some welded joints need it? SAFETY
Post-Weld Heat Treatment relieves residual stress locked into the metal by welding. It's required where wall thickness, material grade, or service stress make residual stress a real risk to long-term integrity — thinner, lower-stress joints elsewhere on the same boiler may not need it.
In IBR terminology, what's the distinction drawn between a "safety valve" and a "relief valve"? SAFETY
"Safety valve" is the term used for steam (compressible fluid) service — it opens fully and rapidly (pop action) once set pressure is reached. "Relief valve" is the term used for liquid service, which tends to open more proportionally to overpressure rather than popping fully open.
Why is feed water deaerated before it reaches the economiser? CHEMISTRY
Dissolved oxygen in feed water causes pitting corrosion on boiler metal at operating temperature — deaeration strips it out mechanically (and a chemical oxygen scavenger typically backs it up) before the water ever sees boiler-side heat.
What is TDS, and why does blowdown control it instead of just treating feed water harder? CHEMISTRY
Total Dissolved Solids — the dissolved mineral content left behind as pure water evaporates to steam. Even well-treated feed water carries some solids, and since only water leaves as steam, those solids concentrate in the drum over time. Blowdown is what actually removes that buildup rather than preventing it from forming.
What causes scale formation inside boiler tubes? CHEMISTRY
Hardness salts (mainly calcium and magnesium compounds) in feed water precipitate out of solution onto the hottest internal tube surfaces. Scale is a poor heat conductor, so it forces tube metal temperature up for the same heat flux — a direct path toward overheating failure.
Beyond corrosion, why else is dissolved oxygen specifically dangerous compared to other dissolved gases? CHEMISTRY
It's an electrochemically active oxidiser at boiler metal temperatures, driving localised pitting rather than uniform corrosion — pitting concentrates metal loss at single points, making it far more likely to cause a through-wall failure than the same total metal loss spread evenly.
